Comments on the marking of Assignment 3

Below is a list of common mistakes that students made in assignment 3. These are the type of errors that would cause the solutions to be marked wrong on an exam.  Please read this list and try to understand why these are mistakes.
  • In 1(a):
    • Some students started the proof from "Assume (n choose k) > (n choose k-1)" and then concluded the statement  was true.
      You need to be very clear (in both your explanation and your own mind) what statement you are assuming and what statement you are concluding (proving)
    • Some students tried to prove by induction, but did not show the induction step, i.e., why if (k-1) is true, then k is true.
      (for those of you who do not know induction, don't worry.  We will learn it in the next chapter).
    • Some students proved the inequality for the case when k = n/2 only and then concluded  that the statement is true.
      If you are asked to prove a statement for a range of values, you must prove it for all of the values in the range,  not just a few of the values.
       
  • In Problem 2, Some students had problems with the meaning of "distinct".
    • "distinct" here means "different".  You can think of each of the chairs as having  a different label or a name
       
  • In Problem 3, A lot of students didn't know how to explain their solution. So they gave examples instead
    • An example is not an explanation.
       
  • In Problem 6,
    • Some students forgot to write a  combinatorial proof.
    • Some students  took "combinatorial proof = prove by example". Then they plugged   numbers and showed the equality. for that example.
      Showing that something is true for a particular example is not a proof that it is always correct.
    • Some students wrote down what the LHS counts and what the RHS counts, but didn't show why the two sides are actually counting the same thing.
      A correct explanation would run something like:  consider counting X;  one way of counting X is to do ... which gives the RHS;  another way of counting X is to do .... which gives the LHS.  Therefore, LHS=RHS.
       
  • In Problem 7, a few students wrote down some algebraic steps to expand the sum and  then "Bang", arrived at the answer without showing how they arrived there.
 

Please note that we have seen a lot of "similar answers" appearing in different papers but no acknowledgements.  Please acknowledge whom you worked with!
