Learning objectives

At the end of the course, you will have achieved the following outcomes.

  1. General
    1. Possess solid understanding of the fundamentals concepts and techniques of AI: search, knowledge representation, machine learning, and natural language processing
    2. Be able to implement using standard functional programming methods of AI
  2. Problem solving as search
    1. Know fundamental techniques for searching state spaces, such as breadth-first, depth-first, best-first, hill-climbing search
    2. Firmly grasp the close relationships of these techniques to fundamental computer science concepts, such as shortest-path algorithms, weighted parsing algorithms, and weighted deductive forward-chaining algorithms
    3. See the connections between greedy, agenda-driven, and dynamic programming formulations of the search techniques, despite their superficial differences
    4. Know why and how the various search techniques possess different strengths and weaknesses
    5. Understand the use of heuristics for search
    6. Understand the properties of admissible heuristics, and the relation of best-first search to A* search
  3. Representing knowledge
    1. Understand concept ontologies
    2. Correctly distinguish between terminological and assertional knowledge
    3. Understand frames and propositions
    4. Be familiar with lexicalized inventories of frames and grasp their significance
    5. Know how to apply forward-chaining search in order to perform inference
    6. Be able to contrast backward-chaining against forward-chaining inference
  4. Machine learning
    1. Know fundamental techniques for machine learning, such as nearest-neighbors, Bayesian models, decision trees
    2. Understand the relationships to neural network models
    3. Be familiar with training and testing methodology
  5. Natural language processing
    1. Understand fundamental concepts of segmentation, tagging, parsing, and syntactic ambiguity
    2. Know core concepts of lexical semantics, including word sense disambiguation and semantic role and frame labeling
    3. Be acquainted with basic ideas of pragmatics, including speech acts and dialog agents
